The Impact of Distorted Thinking

Understanding Cognitive Distortions: How They Skew Your Reality

By Catalina Fernandez - Last updated on September 3, 2024

Imagine your mind as a camera that doesn't capture clear, sharp images but instead takes distorted snapshots of reality. In this mental camera, colors blur and focuses shift, creating a warped version of the world around you. Cognitive distortions are like malfunctioning filters that alter how you view life, making you believe in a version of reality that doesn't always align with the truth. These distortions act as lenses that skew your perception of events, leading to emotional and behavioral responses that can be both dysfunctional and unsatisfactory.

Cognitive distortions manifest through our automatic thoughtsâ€”those ideas that pop into our minds without conscious effort. These distorted thoughts feed negative emotions and, in turn, drive behaviors that may not be the most beneficial or effective. For instance, if you interpret a minor criticism as a total failure, it can lead to feelings of inadequacy and hinder your overall performance. Recognizing these patterns is crucial for developing healthier thought processes and improving emotional well-being.

Types of Cognitive Distortions

  1. Black and White Thinking: This is when we see everything in extremes. Our thinking becomes very absolutist. Everything is either all good or all bad, either a complete success or a total failure. We forget that between black and white, there are many shades of gray.
  2. Emotional Reasoning: This occurs when we use our emotions as evidence of a rational fact. Although our emotions are always valid, they are not always accurate. Often, we base our emotions on a misinterpretation. For example, thinking, "I feel anxious, so there must be real danger," or "I feel like a failure, so I must truly be one." These feelings are taken as objective facts.
  3. Overgeneralization: This happens when we draw broad conclusions based on a single, specific instance with little evidence. A common example is assuming that all future partners will betray you because your ex did. Other comments include, "This always happens," or "I'll never do it well."
  4. "Should" and "Must" Statements: This involves holding rigid expectations about how things or people should be. This distortion is behind many emotional problems and dysfunctions, leading to excessively rigid behavior. Phrases like, "You should work harder," or "I should be more likable," are typical of this type of thinking.
  5. Mental Filters: This occurs when we focus solely on one aspect of a situation, usually a negative one, while ignoring the broader context. For example, if you're a salesperson who meets your targets every month but miss it once, you might consider yourself the worst salesperson without considering that it was a slow season or that you were unwell. It's like a drop of ink that taints an entire glass. You dwell on that one mistake and overlook all the things you did well.
